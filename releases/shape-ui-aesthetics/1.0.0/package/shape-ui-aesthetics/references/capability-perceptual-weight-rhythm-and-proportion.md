# Perceptual Weight, Rhythm & Proportion

**Stable ID:** `perceptual-weight-rhythm-and-proportion`  
**Class:** Core  
**Primary domain:** Spatial Composition

## Observable trigger

Route here when the composition lacks a governing, system-wide target for perceptual mass, tempo, or use-derived proportion and therefore feels inert, top-heavy, monotonous, fragile, or wrongly scaled across regions.

## Non-trigger and near miss

Do not route here for a localized perceived-versus-measured discrepancy after the governing weight, rhythm, and proportion are already accepted; return `reframe` and signal Coherence, Critique & Evidence for controlled diagnosis. Do not route here for incorrect information priority or structural safety.

## Decision question

Which perceptual cues should make the composition feel light, grounded, tense, calm, rhythmic, or proportionate at real viewing distances and activity tempos?

## Interpretation method

Separate actual structure from perceived weight. Thick edges, deep seams, coarse texture, projecting corners, dark values, and deep shadows add visual mass; continuous light surfaces, thin edges, broad pattern, and distance can flatten mass into a plane. Compose rhythm from arrival, pause, turn, comparison, action, and recovery rather than attaching decorative waves or symmetry. Derive proportion from use, content, body, and product consequence.

## Executable design procedure

1. Name the intended weight, stability, tempo, and proportional relation at each consequential viewport and distance.
2. Inventory edge, seam, texture, value, shadow, projection, spacing, and repetition cues.
3. Write the activity score for ordinary, peak, and interrupted use.
4. Assign beats, rests, repetitions, and variations to actual actions and content changes.
5. Derive major ratios from content, use, reach, reading, or protagonist scale; reject arbitrary harmonic numerology.
6. Combine cues consistently and make distance or breakpoint transitions believable.
7. Test optically, in motion, with real content, and after material aging or rendering degradation where relevant.

## Boundaries and failure modes

- Perceptual lightness cannot conceal structural, safety, durability, or fall-risk truth.
- Do not create stage-set material cues that conflict with touch, sound, behavior, or maintenance.
- Do not confuse visual dynamism with bodily rhythm.
- Test more than one life script and avoid proportions derived from a single ideal body or content sample.
- Signal Human Fit for body and access, Information for priority, and Material for evidence conflicts.

## Attached Reference Knowledge

Weight is a multi-cue inference, rhythm is experienced through time, and proportion is a relationship among use, body, content, and form. Optical correction is legitimate when geometry alone produces the wrong perceived relation.
