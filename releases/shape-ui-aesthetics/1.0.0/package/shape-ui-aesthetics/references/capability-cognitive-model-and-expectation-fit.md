# Cognitive Model & Expectation Fit

**Stable ID:** `cognitive-model-and-expectation-fit`  
**Class:** Core  
**Primary domain:** Human Fit, Cognition & Inclusion

## Observable trigger

Route here when a novel aesthetic direction conflicts with learned expectations or the user's goal model; users pass over a visible control because they expect something else; or a redesign abruptly severs a familiar mental model.

## Non-trigger and near miss

Do not route here when users understand the model but dislike the visual style, when the failure is physical target acquisition, or when low contrast alone prevents perception. Copying a competitor's appearance without evidence of user expectation is not a valid familiarity requirement.

## Decision question

Which learned expectations should the design preserve, which deserve to change, and what cues, migration, feedback, and recovery will let users build the new model without weakening the aesthetic proposition?

## Interpretation method

Treat perception as shaped by prior experience, current context, and immediate goals. Reconstruct what users expect to find, where they expect it, what they think it will do, and what result they expect. Locate the exact mismatch between that model and the artifact.

Use familiarity deliberately, not as a mandate for sameness. Preserve conventions that free attention for the core task. Change a convention only when the new model creates consequential product value, then support it through preview, progressive exposure, clear scent, immediate feedback, reversibility, explanation at the moment of need, or temporary parallel paths.

## Executable design procedure

1. Define the representative user's current goal, context, platform history, domain expertise, and relevant cultural or ability variation.
2. Predict expected objects, locations, labels, actions, sequences, and outcomes.
3. Walk the artifact and mark mismatches caused by past experience, current context, or goal filtering.
4. Classify each mismatch as accidental, necessary innovation, or uncertain.
5. Restore familiar cues for accidental mismatches without flattening the thesis.
6. For necessary innovation, design preview, progressive migration, feedback, reversibility, recovery, and concise teaching.
7. Preserve alternate input, reduced-motion, assistive, and localization paths where the new model could exclude.
8. Test whether users can predict the next action and outcome before acting; observe recovery rather than relying on stated preference alone.

## Boundaries and failure modes

- Do not use familiarity to copy category conventions that contradict the product's identity or task.
- Do not assume one culture, age group, platform, language direction, or ability profile defines universal expectation.
- Do not abruptly remove a familiar model when errors carry material cost.
- Do not solve a model mismatch with explanation alone when structure, action, or feedback remains contradictory.
- Signal Interaction when objects and actions are wrong, Information when goal filtering hides evidence, Motion when continuity teaches the new state, and Meaning when the tradeoff is an identity choice.

## Attached Reference Knowledge

Users see what experience, context, and goals prepare them to see. Familiar patterns reduce learning cost but can also perpetuate weak conventions. Rules derived from cognition are hypotheses bounded by audience and context, not universal commands. Preserve novelty where it creates product meaning; repair the expectation path around it.

## Runtime Self-Critique

Ask whether a user can predict the next action and result, whether novelty carries enough value to justify learning, whether transition support is present at the point of need, and whose expectations the design has treated as normal.
