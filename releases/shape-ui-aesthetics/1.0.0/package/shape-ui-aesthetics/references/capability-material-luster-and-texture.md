# Material, Luster & Texture

**Stable ID:** `material-luster-and-texture`  
**Class:** Core  
**Primary domain:** Color, Light & Material

## Observable trigger

Route here when an interface or rendered protagonist needs convincing material behavior, optical life, or controlled surface richness and current surfaces feel generic, noisy, flat, plastic, or inconsistent with their claimed construction.

## Non-trigger and near miss

Do not route here for whole-frame exposure or tone mapping, asset naming, a deliberately flat/unlit language, or exact spectral and engineering material measurement.

## Decision question

What evidence chain across geometry, surface orientation, light, viewpoint, environment, scale, and history makes each material perceptually credible and aesthetically purposeful?

## Interpretation method

Describe the visible failure before naming a shader. Separate object-fixed diffuse and texture evidence from viewpoint-dependent specular and reflection evidence. Check geometry and edge normals before roughness or light. Assign color, roughness/reflection, normal, displacement, transparency, or real geometry only to evidence each representation can produce. Treat wear as contact, gravity, water, traffic, or maintenance history. Separate luster from value: optical shimmer may come from fragmented color, highlights, scale, and distance fusion without making the surface globally lighter.

## Executable design procedure

1. Lock reference, camera, light, output, material role, and desired degree of realism or abstraction.
2. Inventory silhouette, edge rolloff, terminator, highlight shape and movement, reflection content, transmission, texture scale, and wear.
3. Rotate camera or subject to distinguish fixed and viewpoint-dependent evidence.
4. Repair geometry and surface orientation before material channels.
5. Assign each required cue to the least complex valid representation.
6. Supply motivated environment and causal history; remove baked lighting conflicts and random noise.
7. Tune luster scale and viewing distance while preserving the value hierarchy.
8. Validate tiling, aging, multiple lights, motion, fallback, and target devices.

## Boundaries and failure modes

- Do not use bump where silhouette, junction, or reflection path requires geometry.
- Do not embed reusable color maps with obsolete highlight and shadow evidence.
- Do not increase reflectivity in an empty environment or use random wear as authenticity.
- Do not add shimmer where a matte, quiet surface better serves task and thesis.
- Preserve performance and accessible information when material effects degrade.

## Attached Reference Knowledge

Material is inferred from coordinated cues. Edge geometry gives highlights somewhere to exist; reflection requires a world to reflect. Broken color and tiny highlights can create luster at viewing distance while unchanged value keeps solidity.

Ask whether each cue has a physical or authored cause, whether the surface survives camera movement and distance, whether richness competes with content, and whether fallback preserves material distinction without false simulation.
