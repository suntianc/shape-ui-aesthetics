# Motion Purpose & Causality

**Stable ID:** `motion-purpose-and-causality`  
**Class:** Core  
**Primary domain:** Motion, Rhythm & Causality

## Observable trigger

Route here when motion is proposed or present without a clear informational or experiential job, or when users cannot connect an action, state change, outcome, or progress signal through time.

## Non-trigger and near miss

Do not route here when motion already has an accepted temporal job and the unresolved question is whether an implemented hypothesis meets that job under real evidence. If background waiting already has a clear causal model but lacks truthful progress or uncertainty feedback, return `reframe` and signal Motion, Rhythm & Causality to the Director.

## Decision question

Should this motion exist, and if so, what exact causal, orienting, attentional, explanatory, progress, or identity relationship must it make perceptible?

## Interpretation method

Reject “delight,” “modern,” and “energy” as sufficient purposes. Name the user's unknown: what the object is, what an action will do, what changed, where it went, or how far a process has progressed. Select the smallest temporal behavior that resolves that unknown.

Use four causal layers when needed: hint an affordance, cue a next action, preview an effect, and confirm the result. Keep motion spatially and temporally attached to the initiating action. Borrow familiar motion conventions for identity when that reduces explanation; place novelty where semantic risk is low. Preserve context during progress instead of covering useful completed regions.

## Executable design procedure

1. List every proposed or existing motion and name its intended job: orientation, focus, cause/effect, feedback, demonstration, progress, or identity.
2. Remove motion with no observable user or product understanding benefit.
3. For retained motion, identify the initiating event, origin, affected object, state delta, destination or outcome, and reversibility.
4. Choose the necessary causal layer: affordance hint, action cue, effect preview, result confirmation, or truthful progress.
5. Keep the response immediate and local enough for users to connect cause and effect.
6. Allocate novelty and salience according to consequence; do not let identity motion overpower task feedback.
7. Define no-motion and reduced-motion equivalents that preserve state truth, orientation, and completion.
8. Test interruption, repeated activation, slow hardware, keyboard and assistive input, hidden/offscreen targets, and actual loading behavior.

## Boundaries and failure modes

- Do not animate an immediate response when delay weakens clarity.
- Do not use motion to conceal a broken flow, missing feedback, or false progress.
- Do not block input or ignore repeated action while an animation plays.
- Avoid hidden gestures without cues and full-screen waiting that erases usable context.
- Signal Human Fit for comfort and expectation, Interaction for loop repair, Information for competing temporal salience, and Medium when representation controls feasibility.

## Attached Reference Knowledge

Motion can explain identity, outcome, and progress as separate layers. Familiarity reduces interpretation cost, while preview protects agency before commitment. Cause-and-effect motion works only when timing and spatial continuity make the relationship evident. High-frequency, serious, or resource-constrained tools may require very little motion.

Return exactly the canonical seven fields from `routing-and-reconciliation.md`: `decision`, `evidence`, `recommendation`, `consequence`, `boundary`, `validation`, and `onward`. Do not add a mini-report.

Populate those fields with the decision key; motion and observed causal gap; keep/remove decision; named purpose; initiating event and state delta; causal behavior; attention and identity consequence; reduced/no-motion equivalent; technical validation obligations; and the onward state.

## Runtime Self-Critique

Ask whether every retained motion explains something specific, whether cause remains obvious under interruption and reduced motion, whether task feedback wins over spectacle, and whether removing the animation would expose an unresolved product problem.
