# Palette, Value & Relational Color

**Stable ID:** `palette-value-and-relational-color`  
**Class:** Core  
**Primary domain:** Color, Light & Material

## Observable trigger

Route here when a palette needs perceptual hierarchy, value structure, quantity, recurrence, and placement logic rather than a collection of individually attractive swatches.

## Non-trigger and near miss

Do not route here for a single vibrating boundary, an isolated reproduction or color-management defect, a mandated palette with no authority to alter relationships, or a local light/shadow modeling problem. A request for more colorful styling without a product or hierarchy purpose is incomplete framing.

## Decision question

Which relational color system preserves the intended hierarchy, atmosphere, identity, and material separation across real quantities, placements, states, and output conditions?

## Interpretation method

Treat color as a system under shared viewing conditions. Establish the output and the perceived neutral, then describe other surfaces and lights relative to it by value, temperature, chroma, and material response. Choose which value separations must carry form, focus, atmosphere, state, or illumination; sacrifice lesser separations when the medium cannot preserve all of them.

Judge harmony through quantity, recurrence, shape, placement, material, light, reflection, and reading direction, not hue geometry alone. Preserve accent exclusivity. Let environmental color contaminate surfaces only when the light or material relationship motivates it. Test cultural and semantic claims rather than treating associations as universal.

## Executable design procedure

1. Lock the target output conditions, required semantic colors, contrast obligations, and a perceptual neutral.
2. Inventory current colors by role, value, chroma, area, recurrence, placement, and state.
3. Name the palette's value intention and identify separations essential to hierarchy, form, atmosphere, and interaction.
4. Define a small role system for ground, surface, structure, text, muted information, focus, action, status, and exceptional accent.
5. Map relative temperature and chroma relationships; restrict pure extremes unless the thesis intentionally requires them.
6. Test quantity and placement changes before replacing hues.
7. Apply the system to real content and empty/loading/error/success states, then inspect grayscale, contrast, color-vision conditions, target displays, and light/dark contexts as applicable.
8. Record where the system must degrade or where a fixed semantic color constrains aesthetic freedom.

## Boundaries and failure modes

- Never break mandatory safety, scientific, product, or brand semantics for harmony.
- Do not infer universal emotion or cultural meaning from a hue.
- Avoid washing the entire field with the accent; doing so destroys its exclusivity.
- Do not use maximum saturation, pure black, or pure white by habit, but preserve them when an intentional synthetic language requires the extreme.
- Diagnose exposure, white point, gamut, compositing, texture, and material pipeline faults before redesigning the palette.

## Attached Reference Knowledge

Physical value relationships often exceed a medium's range; sensation-coherent translation may preserve decisive separations while compressing others. Equal-area palette diagrams conceal quantity and placement failures. Free studies can discover relationships, but constraints reveal whether those relationships survive product roles. Evaluate perceptual formats and wide gamut only when they change visible control or delivery truth.

## Runtime Self-Critique

Ask whether hierarchy survives in value alone, whether accents remain scarce, whether real area ratios match the intended relation, whether material and state differences remain legible, and which claims require device or audience validation.
