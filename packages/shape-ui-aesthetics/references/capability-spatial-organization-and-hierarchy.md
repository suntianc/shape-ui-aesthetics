# Spatial Organization & Hierarchy

**Stable ID:** `spatial-organization-and-hierarchy`  
**Class:** Core  
**Primary domain:** Spatial Composition

## Observable trigger

Route here when the page lacks a convincing whole, center of gravity, hierarchy of masses, or accepted composition regimes; regions read as unrelated slabs or the intended hierarchy itself is unresolved across conditions.

## Non-trigger and near miss

Do not route here when composition regimes and dominant/subordinate relationships are accepted but implementation accumulates breakpoint patches between them; return `reframe` and signal Computational Medium & Embodiment. If the organization is sound but experience through concealment, approach, or reveal remains unresolved, return `reframe` and signal Spatial Composition.

## Decision question

Which spatial organization best expresses the program and thesis, and how should dominant, subordinate, and linking regions encode value without making every region exceptional?

## Interpretation method

Start from program, not a preferred shape. Classify regions by rigidity, repetition, variability, duration, and public or private consequence. Model whether pairs need containment, interlock, adjacency, separation, or a shared connector. Compare concentrated, linear, radial, clustered, grid, and deliberately hybrid organizations by their ability to carry those relationships.

Build hierarchy by translating value differences into perceptible exceptions against a clear norm. Use scale, position, shape, depth, isolation, or continuity sparingly. A focal region needs subordinate mass and linking structure; emphasis everywhere cancels hierarchy.

## Executable design procedure

1. Inventory required regions, real content, states, and protected controls.
2. Classify each region by rigidity, recurrence, variability, duration, and task consequence.
3. Map pairwise spatial relationships: contain, interlock, adjoin, separate, or connect through a shared region.
4. Compare at least three organization models against program, entry and flow, external constraints, and whole-page silhouette.
5. Select one governing model and assign any hybrid secondary model a specific responsibility.
6. Declare dominant, subordinate, and linking regions; establish the normal spatial rule before creating exceptions.
7. Encode hierarchy with the smallest sufficient set of scale, position, shape, depth, isolation, or continuity differences.
8. Remove labels and test whether the value order remains visible.
9. Recompose at consequential narrow and wide states; preserve relationships rather than coordinates.

## Boundaries and failure modes

- Treat organization types as comparison tools, never style templates.
- Do not infer hierarchy from size alone or create multiple equal centers of gravity accidentally.
- Check whether visual hierarchy hides service users, hardens inappropriate power relations, or places critical recovery outside the dominant path.
- Do not claim responsive success from shrinking; re-evaluate dominance, adjacency, and linking at each composition regime.
- Signal Interaction when spatial organization changes action or navigation, and Human Fit when reach, reading order, or assistive sequence diverges.

## Attached Reference Knowledge

A datum can unify dissimilar elements through a shared edge, axis, field, or rhythm. Atomic hierarchy makes small units inherit the system's organization without flattening their local roles. Architectural prototypes transfer as relationships—center/periphery, threshold, field, procession—not as literal building shapes.
