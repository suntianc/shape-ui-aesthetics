# Accessible & Responsive Motion

**Stable ID:** `accessible-responsive-motion`  
**Class:** Core  
**Primary domain:** Motion, Rhythm & Causality

## Observable trigger

Route here when an accepted motion direction creates a temporal risk: blocking or interruption, vestibular or seizure harm, motion-dependent state, flashing or auto-advance, or failure under target device, viewport, power, and performance conditions.

## Non-trigger and near miss

Do not route here when the unresolved issue is semantic layering, readability, focus, hit testing, or redundant channels of an effect that happens to move; return `reframe` and signal Human Fit, Cognition & Inclusion. When temporal risk and effect-layer access are independently material, include that evidence in the contribution so the Director can form a cross-domain cluster. If motion has no purpose, remove it before designing fallbacks.

## Decision question

How can the primary motion idea survive while every user receives truthful state, control, orientation, and comfort under reduced motion and constrained devices?

## Interpretation method

Audit large movement, parallax, zoom, flashing, auto-advance, duration, interruption, input blocking, property cost, concurrency, and device thresholds. Identify the informational relationship carried by motion, then design reduced, paused, static, or alternative forms that preserve it. Use progressive enhancement: critical task and content exist before optional motion.

## Executable design procedure

1. List motion benefits and risk surfaces by event and user condition.
2. Classify essential state information separately from expressive movement.
3. Define reduced-motion, no-motion, pause/stop, keyboard, and screen-reader equivalents.
4. Make animations interruptible; preserve input and final state under repeated activation.
5. Choose lower-impact properties and budget peak concurrent animation.
6. Establish thresholds from real devices and degrade expensive layers gracefully.
7. Test focus, zoom/reflow, context loss, backgrounding, power constraints, reduced-motion settings, and current accessibility standards.

## Boundaries and failure modes

- Never make critical information or task completion depend on animation.
- Avoid flashing, involuntary auto-advance, unbounded parallax, and unpausable motion.
- Do not interpret `prefers-reduced-motion` as permission to remove state feedback.
- Do not block controls until a flourish finishes.
- Verify current standards and platform behavior rather than historical performance assumptions.

## Attached Reference Knowledge

Motion can aid orientation and feedback while harming vestibular, migraine, seizure, motor-control, or assistive-technology users. Accessible alternatives preserve the relationship, not necessarily the movement. Performance degradation should remove decorative cost before state truth.

## Runtime Self-Critique

Ask what fails when animation is absent, whether input stays available, whether reduced motion preserves meaning, and whether real low-capability devices receive a coherent rather than broken experience.
